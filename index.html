01

What Is a Cell?

Every living thing, from the bacteria in your yogurt to the blue whale, is made of cells. A cell is the smallest unit that can carry out all the activities we associate with life: it can grow, respond to its environment, use energy, and reproduce. Think of a cell as a tiny, self-contained factory. It takes in raw materials, processes them, builds products, and disposes of waste, all within a space far too small to see with the naked eye.

This idea is captured in cell theory, one of the foundational principles of biology. Cell theory says three things: all living organisms are made of one or more cells, the cell is the basic unit of life, and new cells arise only from existing cells. There is no "spontaneous generation" of life from non-living matter; every cell you have right now descended, through an unbroken chain of divisions, from older cells.

Example
Your body contains roughly 37 trillion cells. A single drop of blood carries about 5 million red blood cells. Meanwhile, some organisms, like amoebas, are just one cell that does everything on its own.
Key Takeaway
The cell is the smallest thing that is alive. Everything living is made of cells, and every cell comes from a pre-existing cell.
Common Mistake
Viruses are not cells. They lack the machinery to reproduce on their own and must hijack a host cell. On an exam, do not call viruses "living cells."
TermDefinition
CellThe basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.
Cell TheoryThe principle that all living things are made of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, and all cells come from pre-existing cells.
OrganismAny individual living thing, whether made of one cell or trillions.
UnicellularAn organism made of only one cell (e.g., bacteria, yeast).
MulticellularAn organism made of many cells working together (e.g., humans, trees).
02

Prokaryotes vs. Eukaryotes

Not all cells are built the same way. The most fundamental divide in cell biology is between cells that keep their DNA loose inside the cell and cells that package their DNA inside a special compartment called a nucleus. Cells without a nucleus are called prokaryotes (the word means "before the nucleus"), and cells with a nucleus are called eukaryotes ("true nucleus"). Bacteria and archaea are prokaryotes. Animals, plants, fungi, and protists are all eukaryotes.

Prokaryotic cells are generally much smaller and simpler. They still have a cell membrane and ribosomes (tiny machines that build proteins), but they lack the complex internal compartments, called organelles, that eukaryotic cells have. Eukaryotic cells are larger and more elaborate, with distinct membrane-bound organelles that each perform specialized jobs, much like departments within a company.

Example
The bacterium E. coli in your gut is a prokaryote: small, no nucleus, relatively simple. A white blood cell defending your body is a eukaryote: larger, nucleus-containing, packed with specialized organelles.
Key Takeaway
The defining difference is the nucleus. Prokaryotes = no nucleus. Eukaryotes = nucleus. This is the biggest structural division in the world of cells.
Common Mistake
"Prokaryotes have no DNA" is wrong. They absolutely have DNA; it just floats freely in the cytoplasm rather than being enclosed in a nucleus.
TermDefinition
ProkaryoteA cell that lacks a membrane-bound nucleus. Examples: bacteria, archaea.
EukaryoteA cell that contains a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles. Examples: animal, plant, fungal cells.
NucleusA membrane-enclosed compartment that houses the cell's DNA.
OrganelleA specialized structure inside a cell that performs a specific function (literally "little organ").
RibosomeA tiny structure (not membrane-bound) that builds proteins by reading genetic instructions.
03

The Cell Membrane

Every cell is wrapped in a thin, flexible boundary called the cell membrane (also known as the plasma membrane). Its job is to control what enters and exits the cell, acting as a selective gatekeeper. Without a membrane, a cell's contents would simply mix with the surrounding fluid and the cell would cease to exist as a distinct unit.

The membrane is built mainly from phospholipids, molecules that have a water-loving (hydrophilic) head and two water-fearing (hydrophobic) tails. In water, these molecules naturally arrange themselves into a double layer, the phospholipid bilayer, with the heads facing outward toward water and the tails tucked inward, away from water. Scattered throughout this bilayer are proteins that serve as channels, receptors, and identity markers, as well as cholesterol molecules that help keep the membrane fluid but stable.

Scientists often describe this arrangement with the fluid mosaic model: "fluid" because the phospholipids and proteins drift and move within the layer like boats on a lake, and "mosaic" because the variety of proteins and lipids creates a patchwork pattern.

Example
Imagine a soap bubble. The thin film of the bubble is a bit like a cell membrane: it forms a boundary, it is flexible, and it separates the inside from the outside. Now imagine that bubble has tiny doors and windows (proteins) built into it to let certain things in or out.
Key Takeaway
The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer studded with proteins. It is selectively permeable, meaning it controls which substances pass through.
Common Mistake
Do not confuse the cell membrane with the cell wall. The membrane is a flexible lipid bilayer found in ALL cells. The cell wall is a rigid outer layer found only in plants, fungi, and bacteria, located outside the membrane.
TermDefinition
Cell MembraneThe thin, flexible barrier surrounding every cell; controls what enters and exits.
PhospholipidA lipid molecule with a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails; the main building block of the membrane.
Phospholipid BilayerThe two-layer arrangement of phospholipids that forms the cell membrane.
Selectively PermeableAllowing some substances to pass through while blocking others.
Fluid Mosaic ModelThe accepted description of membrane structure: a fluid layer of lipids with a mosaic of embedded proteins.
04

The Nucleus

If the cell is a factory, the nucleus is the front office where the master blueprints are stored. The nucleus houses nearly all of the cell's DNA, organized into structures called chromosomes. These chromosomes contain the genes, the specific instructions for building every protein the cell needs. The nucleus does not build the proteins itself; instead it sends out messenger copies of the instructions (messenger RNA) to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm, which do the actual construction.

Surrounding the nucleus is a double membrane called the nuclear envelope, which is punctuated by nuclear pores, tiny channels that allow molecules like mRNA and proteins to pass back and forth between the nucleus and the rest of the cell. Inside the nucleus, you can sometimes see a dense region called the nucleolus, which is where the cell builds ribosomal RNA, a key component of ribosomes.

Example
Think of the nucleus as a library's restricted archive. The books (DNA) never leave, but photocopies of specific pages (mRNA) are sent out through the checkout desk (nuclear pores) so the factory floor (ribosomes) can follow the instructions.
Key Takeaway
The nucleus stores and protects the cell's DNA and controls gene expression by sending mRNA instructions to the cytoplasm.
Common Mistake
Not all eukaryotic cells have a nucleus at all times. Mature red blood cells in mammals lose their nucleus to make more room for hemoglobin. Do not assume nucleus = present in every human cell.
TermDefinition
ChromosomeA structure of tightly coiled DNA and proteins found in the nucleus.
GeneA segment of DNA that contains the instructions for building one protein (or RNA).
Nuclear EnvelopeThe double membrane surrounding the nucleus.
Nuclear PoreAn opening in the nuclear envelope that regulates transport in and out of the nucleus.
NucleolusA dense region within the nucleus where ribosomal RNA is produced.
mRNAMessenger RNA; a copy of a gene's instructions that travels from the nucleus to the ribosome.
05

Organelles: The Cell's Machinery

Eukaryotic cells contain a collection of membrane-bound organelles, each with a distinct role, floating in a jelly-like substance called the cytoplasm. Running through the cytoplasm is a network of internal membranes called the endomembrane system, which includes the endoplasmic reticulum and the Golgi apparatus and works like an assembly line.

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The ER is a sprawling network of membrane-enclosed channels connected to the nuclear envelope. It comes in two forms. Rough ER is studded with ribosomes on its surface, giving it a bumpy appearance; its job is to fold and process proteins destined for membranes or export out of the cell. Smooth ER lacks ribosomes and specializes in lipid synthesis, detoxification of drugs and poisons, and calcium storage.

Golgi Apparatus

After proteins are made on the rough ER, they travel in small transport bubbles (vesicles) to the Golgi apparatus, a stack of flattened membrane sacs that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins for their final destinations, whether that is another organelle, the cell surface, or secretion outside the cell. Think of the Golgi as the post office of the cell.

Lysosomes

Lysosomes are membrane-bound sacs filled with digestive enzymes. They break down worn-out organelles, food particles, and invading bacteria. They keep the rest of the cell safe by containing these harsh enzymes inside their own membrane.

Cytoskeleton

The cytoskeleton is a network of protein fibers that gives the cell its shape, helps it move, and provides tracks for organelles to travel along. It includes microfilaments (thin, for movement), intermediate filaments (strong, for support), and microtubules (thick, for transport and cell division).

Example
Imagine the cell as an Amazon warehouse. The rough ER is the manufacturing area where items (proteins) are assembled. The Golgi is the packing and shipping department, adding labels and sorting parcels. Lysosomes are the recycling and waste disposal crews. The cytoskeleton is the conveyor belt system moving everything around.
Key Takeaway
The rough ER makes proteins, the Golgi modifies and ships them, and lysosomes digest waste. These organelles work together as an interconnected system.
Common Mistake
Students often confuse the rough ER and the Golgi. The ER makes and folds; the Golgi modifies and sorts. Proteins go ER → Golgi → destination, not the other way around.
TermDefinition
CytoplasmThe jelly-like material filling the cell between the membrane and the nucleus.
Rough EREndoplasmic reticulum studded with ribosomes; synthesizes and folds proteins.
Smooth EREndoplasmic reticulum without ribosomes; makes lipids and detoxifies chemicals.
Golgi ApparatusStacked membrane sacs that modify, sort, and package proteins.
LysosomeOrganelle containing digestive enzymes that breaks down waste and debris.
VesicleA small membrane-bound sac that transports materials within the cell.
CytoskeletonNetwork of protein fibers providing shape, support, and movement to the cell.
06

Energy and the Mitochondria

Cells need energy to do everything: build molecules, move, divide, send signals. The main energy currency of the cell is a molecule called ATP (adenosine triphosphate). You can think of ATP as a rechargeable battery. When the cell needs energy, it breaks off one phosphate group from ATP, releasing energy and leaving behind ADP (adenosine diphosphate). The cell then "recharges" ADP back into ATP.

The organelle primarily responsible for manufacturing ATP in eukaryotic cells is the mitochondrion (plural: mitochondria). Mitochondria take in glucose-derived molecules and oxygen and, through a multi-step process called cellular respiration, produce large quantities of ATP. The simplified overall equation is: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP.

Mitochondria have their own double membrane: a smooth outer membrane and a highly folded inner membrane. The folds, called cristae, increase the surface area available for the chemical reactions that generate ATP. Interestingly, mitochondria contain their own small DNA molecule, which is evidence supporting the endosymbiotic theory, the idea that mitochondria were once free-living bacteria that were engulfed by an ancestral cell billions of years ago.

Example
Your muscle cells, which demand a lot of energy during exercise, are packed with thousands of mitochondria. A less active cell, like a skin cell, has far fewer. The number of mitochondria in a cell reflects its energy needs.
Key Takeaway
Mitochondria are the powerhouses of the cell. They convert glucose and oxygen into ATP through cellular respiration.
Common Mistake
Cellular respiration is NOT the same as breathing (though breathing supplies the oxygen). Respiration happens inside cells at the mitochondria. Breathing is just the physical act of getting oxygen into your lungs and bloodstream.
TermDefinition
ATPAdenosine triphosphate; the cell's primary energy carrier molecule.
MitochondrionThe organelle that produces most of the cell's ATP through cellular respiration.
Cellular RespirationThe process of breaking down glucose with oxygen to produce ATP, CO₂, and water.
CristaeThe folds of the inner mitochondrial membrane that increase surface area for ATP production.
Endosymbiotic TheoryThe hypothesis that mitochondria (and chloroplasts) originated as independent bacteria engulfed by an ancestral eukaryotic cell.
07

Plant Cells vs. Animal Cells

Plant cells and animal cells are both eukaryotic, so they share most of the same organelles: nucleus, mitochondria, ER, Golgi, ribosomes, and cytoskeleton. However, plant cells have three major structures that animal cells lack, and understanding these differences is essential.

First, plant cells have a rigid cell wall made of cellulose that sits outside the cell membrane. This wall gives plants their structural support (it is why a tree can stand upright without a skeleton). Second, plant cells contain chloroplasts, the organelles that carry out photosynthesis, using sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Like mitochondria, chloroplasts have their own DNA and double membrane, further evidence for the endosymbiotic theory. Third, plant cells typically have a large central vacuole that stores water, nutrients, and waste products and helps maintain the cell's shape by exerting pressure against the cell wall.

Example
When a houseplant wilts, it is because the central vacuoles in its cells have lost water. Without that water pressure pushing outward against the cell walls, the stems and leaves go limp. Watering the plant refills the vacuoles and restores the rigid, upright structure.
Key Takeaway
Plant cells have a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole. Animal cells have none of these three. Both types have mitochondria.
Common Mistake
"Plant cells do photosynthesis, so they don't need mitochondria." Wrong! Plant cells DO have mitochondria and perform cellular respiration, especially at night when photosynthesis stops. They need both organelles.
TermDefinition
Cell WallA rigid outer layer (cellulose in plants) that provides structural support outside the membrane.
ChloroplastAn organelle in plant (and algal) cells that converts sunlight into glucose through photosynthesis.
PhotosynthesisThe process of using light energy to make glucose from CO₂ and water.
Central VacuoleA large, water-filled organelle in plant cells that stores materials and helps maintain cell shape.
Turgor PressureThe pressure exerted by water inside the vacuole pushing against the cell wall, keeping the plant firm.

Cell Division: Making New Cells

Growth, repair, and reproduction all depend on cells being able to copy themselves. The process by which a single cell divides into two genetically identical daughter cells is called mitosis (together with cytokinesis, the physical splitting of the cytoplasm). Before a cell divides, it must first duplicate all of its DNA so that each new cell receives a complete copy of the genetic instructions. This DNA duplication happens during a phase called S phase, which is part of the larger cell cycle.

The cell cycle has two broad stages: interphase (the cell grows, copies its DNA, and prepares to divide) and the mitotic phase (the cell actually divides). Interphase is the longest part of the cycle and includes G1 (growth), S (DNA synthesis), and G2 (preparation for mitosis). Mitosis itself has four sub-stages: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, during which the copied chromosomes are carefully sorted and pulled to opposite ends of the cell before it splits in two.

Example
When you scrape your knee, your body heals the wound through mitosis. Skin cells at the edges of the cut rapidly divide, producing new cells to fill in the gap. Each new cell is genetically identical to the ones that were there before.
Key Takeaway
Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells and is used for growth and repair. DNA must be copied before the cell divides.
Common Mistake
Do not confuse mitosis with meiosis. Mitosis produces two identical cells (for growth/repair). Meiosis produces four genetically unique cells (for making sex cells/gametes). At the basic level, focus on mitosis.
TermDefinition
Cell CycleThe sequence of growth, DNA replication, and division that a cell goes through.
InterphaseThe portion of the cell cycle where the cell grows and copies its DNA (G1, S, G2).
MitosisThe division of the nucleus resulting in two genetically identical nuclei.
CytokinesisThe physical division of the cytoplasm that follows mitosis, producing two separate cells.
Daughter CellsThe two new cells produced by cell division; genetically identical to each other and to the parent.
ChromosomeA structure of DNA and protein; must be duplicated before cell division.

How Molecules Move In and Out

The cell membrane is selectively permeable, but how exactly do molecules get across it? There are two broad categories: passive transport (no energy required) and active transport (energy, usually ATP, required).

Diffusion is the simplest form of passive transport. Molecules naturally move from an area of higher concentration to an area of lower concentration, spreading out until they are evenly distributed. Small, nonpolar molecules like oxygen and carbon dioxide can diffuse directly through the phospholipid bilayer. When water specifically moves across a selectively permeable membrane by diffusion, it is called osmosis. Larger or charged molecules may need help from membrane proteins, a process called facilitated diffusion, which is still passive because it follows the concentration gradient.

Sometimes the cell needs to move substances against the concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), which requires energy. This is active transport. An important example is the sodium-potassium pump, which uses ATP to pump sodium ions out of the cell and potassium ions in, maintaining the electrical balance the cell depends on.

Example
When you spray perfume in one corner of a room, you can eventually smell it across the room. That spreading is diffusion. Osmosis works the same way, but specifically for water moving through a membrane. If you put a raisin in water, it plumps up because water moves in by osmosis.
Key Takeaway
Passive transport (diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion) requires no energy and moves substances down their concentration gradient. Active transport uses ATP to move substances against the gradient.
Common Mistake
Facilitated diffusion is passive, NOT active, even though it uses a protein channel. The key question is: does it require energy (ATP)? If no, it is passive. The protein just provides a pathway; it does not supply energy.
TermDefinition
DiffusionThe movement of molecules from high to low concentration; no energy needed.
OsmosisThe diffusion of water across a selectively permeable membrane.
Facilitated DiffusionPassive transport of molecules through membrane proteins, following the gradient.
Active TransportMovement of molecules against the concentration gradient, requiring ATP energy.
Concentration GradientThe difference in concentration of a substance between two areas.

Putting It All Together

A cell is not just a bag of independent parts. It is an integrated system where every component depends on the others. The nucleus holds the instructions; ribosomes read those instructions to build proteins; the ER folds and processes those proteins; the Golgi packages and ships them; the mitochondria supply the ATP energy that powers all of this work; the cytoskeleton moves materials from place to place; and the cell membrane controls what gets in and what gets out. Remove any one piece and the system breaks down.

Understanding cells at the basic level means grasping this interconnectedness. When a cell receives a signal from outside (say, a hormone binding to a receptor on the membrane), that signal can trigger the nucleus to turn on specific genes, which leads to new proteins being made, which might cause the cell to divide, change shape, or release a chemical signal of its own. Biology at this scale is about communication and coordination, thousands of molecular events happening in concert every second inside a space smaller than the period at the end of this sentence.

Example
When you eat a meal, the hormone insulin binds to receptors on your cell membranes, triggering a signal inside the cell that ultimately causes glucose transporter proteins to move to the membrane surface so glucose can enter. This involves the membrane, signaling proteins, gene expression, and energy use, all working together.
Key Takeaway
The cell is an integrated system. Organelles do not work in isolation; they cooperate in pathways that keep the cell alive, responsive, and functional.
